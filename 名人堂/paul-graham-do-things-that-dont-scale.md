# Do Things That Don't Scale

**Author:** Paul Graham
**Date:** July 2013
**Source:** paulgraham.com
**Category:** Startups / Growth / Strategy

---

## TL;DR

Startups don't take off by themselves — founders make them take off. The most important unscalable thing is **recruiting users one by one** and **making them insanely happy**. Stripe invented "Collison installation" — when someone agreed to try it, they'd say "give me your laptop" and set them up on the spot. Airbnb went door to door in New York. Facebook started as Harvard-only. Think of startup ideas not as scalars but as **vectors**: what you build + the unscalable thing you do to ignite it.

---

## Recruit — The Collison Installation

Nearly all startups have to **recruit users manually** at the start. You can't wait for users to come to you.

Stripe is one of the most successful startups YC has funded. At YC we use the term **"Collison installation"** for the technique they invented. More diffident founders ask "Will you try our beta?" and if the answer is yes, they say "Great, we'll send you a link." But the Collison brothers weren't going to wait. When anyone agreed to try Stripe they'd say **"Right then, give me your laptop"** and set them up on the spot.

Two reasons founders resist recruiting individually:

1. **Shyness and laziness.** They'd rather sit at home writing code than go out and talk to strangers and probably be rejected by most of them.
2. **The absolute numbers seem so small.** They think this can't be how big startups got started.

The mistake is to **underestimate the power of compound growth**. If you have 100 users and grow 10% a week, after a year you'll have 14,000 users, and after 2 years you'll have 2 million.

**Airbnb** is a classic example. Marketplaces are so hard to get rolling that you should expect to take **heroic measures** at first. In Airbnb's case, these consisted of going door to door in New York, recruiting new users and helping existing ones improve their listings. When I remember the Airbnbs during YC, I picture them with **rolly bags**, because when they showed up for tuesday dinners they'd always just flown back from somewhere.

## Fragile — Every Startup Is a Newborn

Airbnb now seems like an unstoppable juggernaut, but early on it was so fragile that **about 30 days of going out and engaging in person** with users made the difference between success and failure.

Inexperienced founders and investors unconsciously **judge larval startups by the standards of established ones**. They're like someone looking at a newborn baby and concluding "there's no way this tiny creature could ever accomplish anything."

The big danger is that **you'll dismiss your startup yourself**. Even Bill Gates made that mistake — he returned to Harvard after starting Microsoft. He wouldn't have returned at all if he'd realized Microsoft was going to be even a fraction of the size it turned out to be.

> The question to ask about an early stage startup is not "is this company taking over the world?" but **"how big could this company get if the founders did the right things?"**

And the right things often seem **both laborious and inconsequential** at the time.

## Delight — Insanely Great Experience

You should take extraordinary measures not just to acquire users, but also to **make them happy**. Wufoo sent each new user a **hand-written thank you note**. Your first users should feel that signing up with you was one of the best choices they ever made.

Three reasons founders don't focus enough on this:

1. **Engineers aren't trained for customer service.** You can be ornery when you're Scotty, but not when you're Kirk.
2. **They worry it won't scale.** But in their current state they have nothing to lose. That would be a great problem to have.
3. **They've never experienced such attention themselves.** Tim Cook doesn't send you a hand-written note after you buy a laptop. He can't. But you can. **That's one advantage of being small.**

> I have never once seen a startup lured down a blind alley by trying too hard to make their initial users happy.

## Experience — It's Not the Product, It's the User

Steve Jobs's "insanely great" — he meant it **more literally** than as a synonym for "very." One should focus on quality of execution to a degree that in everyday life would be considered pathological.

But in the first couple months of a startup's life: **it's not the product that should be insanely great, but the experience of being your user.** You can and should give users an insanely great experience with an early, incomplete, buggy product, if you **make up the difference with attentiveness**.

Over-engaging with early users is not just permissible — it's a **necessary part of the feedback loop** that makes the product good. Perfectionism is often an excuse for procrastination.

> The feedback you get from engaging directly with your earliest users will be **the best you ever get**. When you're so big you have to resort to focus groups, you'll wish you could go over to your users' homes and offices.

## Fire — The Contained Fire Strategy

Sometimes the right trick is to focus on a **deliberately narrow market**. It's like keeping a fire contained at first to get it really hot before adding more logs.

**Facebook** — at first just for Harvard students. Only a few thousand potential users, but because they felt it was really for them, a **critical mass signed up**. Mark Zuckerberg said creating course lists for each school was a lot of work, but doing that made students feel the site was their natural home.

Among companies, the best early adopters are usually **other startups**. They're more open to new things both by nature and because they haven't made all their choices yet.

## Meraki — Pull a Meraki

For hardware startups: **assemble it yourself** at first.

**Pebble** assembled the first several hundred watches themselves. If they hadn't gone through that phase, they probably wouldn't have sold $10 million worth on Kickstarter.

Fabricating things yourself turns out to be valuable. You can **tweak the design faster** when you're the factory. Eric Migicovsky of Pebble: **"how valuable it was to source good screws."** Who knew?

## Consult — Be the Software

Pick a single user and act as if you were **consultants building something just for that one user**. Keep tweaking till you fit their needs perfectly, and you'll usually find you've made something other users want too.

More extreme variant: **don't just use your software, be your software.** When you only have a small number of users, you can sometimes do by hand things that you plan to automate later.

**Stripe** delivered "instant" merchant accounts by having the founders **manually sign up users** for traditional merchant accounts behind the scenes. The technique starts to have aspects of a **practical joke**.

## Big Launch = Usually Doesn't Work

I occasionally meet founders who seem to believe startups are **projectiles rather than powered aircraft**. They want to launch simultaneously in 8 publications, with embargoes. And on a tuesday, of course.

> All you need from a launch is some initial core of users. How well you're doing a few months later will depend more on **how happy you made those users** than how many there were of them.

Partnerships too usually don't work. Six months later they're all saying the same thing: **"that was way more work than we expected, and we ended up getting practically nothing out of it."**

## Vector — Ideas as Pairs

Stop thinking of startup ideas as scalars. Think of them as **vectors**: what you're going to build + the unscalable thing you're going to do initially to get the company going.

In the best case, both components contribute to your company's **DNA**: the unscalable things you have to do to get started change the company permanently for the better.

> If you have to work hard to delight users when you only have a handful of them, **you'll keep doing it when you have a lot**.
