Startups don't take off by themselves — founders make them take off. Recruit users one by one. Make them insanely happy. Think of ideas as vectors, not scalars.

---

## Recruit — The Collison Installation

Nearly all startups have to **recruit users manually** at the start. You can't wait for users to come to you.

At YC we use the term **"Collison installation"** for the technique Stripe invented. More diffident founders ask "Will you try our beta?" and if yes, say "Great, we'll send you a link." But the Collison brothers weren't going to wait. When anyone agreed to try Stripe they'd say **"Right then, give me your laptop"** and set them up on the spot.

Two reasons founders resist this:

1. **Shyness + laziness.** They'd rather write code than talk to strangers.
2. **The numbers seem too small.** They underestimate **compound growth** — 100 users at 10% weekly = 14,000 in a year, 2 million in two.

**Airbnb** went door to door in New York. When I remember the Airbnbs during YC, I picture them with **rolly bags** — they'd always just flown back from somewhere.

## Fragile — Every Startup Is a Newborn

Airbnb now seems unstoppable, but early on **about 30 days of in-person engagement** made the difference between success and failure.

People unconsciously **judge larval startups by established-company standards**. Like looking at a newborn and concluding "this tiny creature could never accomplish anything."

The biggest danger is that **you'll dismiss your startup yourself**. Even Bill Gates returned to Harvard after starting Microsoft.

> The question is not "is this company taking over the world?" but **"how big could it get if the founders did the right things?"**

The right things often seem **both laborious and inconsequential** at the time.

## Delight — You Can, Tim Cook Can't

Take extraordinary measures to **make users happy**. Wufoo sent each new user a **hand-written thank you note**.

> Tim Cook doesn't send you a hand-written note after you buy a laptop. He can't. **But you can. That's one advantage of being small.**

> I have never once seen a startup lured down a blind alley by trying too hard to make their initial users happy.

## Experience — Not the Product, the User

Steve Jobs's "insanely great" — he meant it **literally**, not as a synonym for "very." Focus on quality to a degree that would be considered **pathological**.

In the first months: **it's not the product that should be insanely great, but the experience of being your user.** You can give users an insanely great experience with an early, incomplete, buggy product, if you **make up the difference with attentiveness**.

> The feedback from your earliest users will be **the best you ever get**.

## Fire — Contained Burn

Focus on a **deliberately narrow market** first. Keep the fire contained to get it really hot before adding logs.

**Facebook** — Harvard only. Few thousand potential users, but they felt it was **really for them**, so critical mass signed up.

## Meraki — Assemble It Yourself

**Pebble** assembled the first several hundred watches themselves. If they hadn't, they probably wouldn't have sold $10M on Kickstarter.

Eric Migicovsky: **"how valuable it was to source good screws."** Who knew?

## Consult — Be the Software

Pick a single user. Build as if you're **consultants for that one user**. Keep tweaking till perfect. You'll usually find other users want it too.

**Stripe** delivered "instant" merchant accounts by having founders **manually sign up users behind the scenes**. The technique has aspects of a **practical joke**.

## Big Launch = Usually Doesn't Work

Founders believe startups are **projectiles rather than powered aircraft**. But all you need from a launch is **some initial core of users**.

> How well you're doing months later depends more on **how happy you made those users** than how many there were.

## Vector — Ideas as Pairs

Stop thinking of ideas as scalars. Think of them as **vectors**: what you build + the unscalable thing you do to ignite it.

Both components should become **company DNA**. If you have to work hard to delight users when you only have a handful, **you'll keep doing it when you have a lot**.
